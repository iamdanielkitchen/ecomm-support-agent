# Wool sourcing and certifications

Our Wool Area Rug uses fiber from farms certified under the Responsible Wool Standard. That means the sheep are treated humanely, the land is managed to prevent overgrazing, and the supply chain is traceable from pasture to finished textile. Certified wool typically costs 15–25% more than conventional fiber, but it gives you transparency about animal welfare and environmental impact.

## What RWS certification covers

The Responsible Wool Standard (RWS) is an independent certification administered by the Textile Exchange. Farms earning RWS status must meet requirements in three areas:

- **Animal welfare**: Access to pasture, shelter from extreme weather, and veterinary care. Practices like mulesing (a painful skin-removal technique) are prohibited.
- **Land management**: Ranchers follow rotational grazing plans to protect soil health and native vegetation. The standard also requires biodiversity protections and limits pesticide use.
- **Chain of custody**: Every handler — from shearing shed to processor to dye house — tracks RWS fiber separately from conventional wool, ensuring what you buy actually came from a certified source.

Third-party auditors inspect farms and processors annually. If a supplier falls out of compliance, they lose the certification until corrective action is verified.

## Where the fiber originates

The wool in our 5x7 rug (FG-HOM-002) comes from ranches in New Zealand and Tasmania. Both regions have long wool-growing traditions and strong regulatory frameworks for animal husbandry. We work with a single mill in New Zealand that sources only RWS fiber and handles dyeing, spinning, and weaving in one location. That consolidation reduces transport emissions and gives us tighter quality control than a multi-stage global supply chain.

The finished rugs ship to our warehouse in the continental U.S., where they're inspected and stored flat to preserve the weave. For sizing recommendations and room-layout tips, see our Rug sizes and fit guide.

## Why certified wool costs more

RWS-certified fiber commands a premium because the certification itself has costs — audit fees, separate handling at the mill, smaller production runs — and because responsible land management typically yields less wool per acre than intensive grazing. You're also paying for traceability. Conventional wool often mixes fiber from dozens of farms across multiple countries, making it impossible to verify how the animals were treated or whether the land was degraded in the process.

We absorb some of that premium, but a rug made from certified wool will always cost more than one using untraced fiber. The trade-off is confidence that your purchase supports ranchers who meet higher welfare and environmental standards.

## FAQ

**Does RWS certification mean the wool is organic?**  
No. Organic certification covers pesticide and fertilizer use on pasture, plus restrictions on medications given to sheep. RWS focuses on animal welfare and land stewardship but does not prohibit synthetic inputs. Some farms hold both certifications, but ours currently meet RWS standards only.

**Can I request wool from a specific country or ranch?**  
We source from a single mill that aggregates fiber from multiple RWS ranches in New Zealand and Tasmania. We cannot trace an individual rug back to one farm, but the mill provides quarterly supply-chain reports that list all certified suppliers for that production window.

**What happens to wool that doesn't meet certification standards?**  
It enters the conventional wool market, where it's sold without welfare or land-management claims. RWS farms can produce both certified and non-certified fiber if only part of their operation meets the standard — the two streams are kept separate through the supply chain.
