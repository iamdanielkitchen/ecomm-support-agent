# How our ceramics are made

Every ceramic piece in the Fieldstone catalog starts as raw stoneware clay and goes through a controlled firing process that produces smooth, durable surfaces. We inspect each piece under raking light before it leaves the studio, catching micro-cracks and glaze defects that compromise performance. This article explains the clay body we use, the kiln temperature that creates strength, and the quality check that ensures you receive ceramics built to last.

## The stoneware clay body

Fieldstone ceramics use a mid-range stoneware formulation with a tight particle structure. Stoneware fires denser than earthenware, which means lower water absorption and better resistance to thermal shock. The raw clay arrives as dry powder, mixed with water to a workable consistency, then aged for several weeks to improve plasticity. Aging allows water molecules to penetrate evenly, which reduces warping during the first firing.

Our Ceramic Mixing Bowl Set (FG-KIT-003) and Espresso Cups (FG-KIT-004) both use this stoneware body. The clay's natural iron content gives the unglazed foot a warm taupe color. That unglazed ring also protects countertops and table surfaces from glaze abrasion.

## Kiln firing at Cone 6

All Fieldstone ceramics fire to Cone 6, approximately 2,232°F. This temperature fully vitrifies the clay body, meaning the particles fuse into a glass-like matrix without melting the piece into slag. Vitrification is what makes stoneware non-porous and dishwasher-safe. The firing cycle takes fourteen hours: a slow ramp to avoid thermal shock, a twenty-minute hold at peak temperature, and a controlled cool-down that prevents dunting cracks.

Glaze application happens before the final firing. We use food-safe glazes that mature at the same Cone 6 temperature, creating a permanent bond between glaze and clay. The glaze layer seals the surface and provides the smooth finish you see on mixing bowls and espresso cups. For more on how that glaze holds up in a dishwasher, see our guide on ceramic cookware and dishwasher use.

## Quality inspection for micro-cracks

After the kiln cools, each piece goes through a visual and acoustic inspection. An inspector examines the piece under angled LED light, which casts shadows into hairline cracks invisible under direct overhead light. Raking light reveals glaze crawling, pinholing, and S-cracks that form during cooling.

The inspector also taps the rim with a wooden dowel. A clear ring indicates structural integrity. A dull thud means an internal crack, and that piece does not ship. Micro-cracks weaken ceramics and can widen with thermal cycling, eventually causing a sudden break. This inspection step is why defect returns are rare, but if a piece does arrive damaged, our standard return window applies.

## Why firing temperature matters to you

Cone 6 stoneware sits in a functional sweet spot. It is strong enough for daily use, dishwasher-safe, and less brittle than high-fire porcelain. You can move a Fieldstone mixing bowl from refrigerator to counter without worrying about thermal shock. The vitrified body does not absorb food oils or stains, which keeps the ceramic hygienic over years of use.

## FAQ

**Can I put Fieldstone ceramics in the oven?**  
Yes. Cone 6 stoneware is oven-safe up to 450°F. Avoid direct stovetop heat and do not place a cold ceramic dish into a preheated oven—let it warm gradually to prevent shock cracks.

**Why does the bottom of my bowl feel rough?**  
The unglazed foot ring prevents glaze-on-glaze scratching when bowls are stacked. That raw stoneware texture is intentional and will not damage surfaces.

**Do you offer replacement pieces for broken items in a set?**  
We do not sell individual replacements. If a piece from the Ceramic Mixing Bowl Set arrives damaged, you can return the complete set within thirty days for a refund.
