# Cast iron: seasoning and daily care

Your Cast Iron Skillet, 10" (FG-KIT-001) ships with a factory seasoning layer already baked in, so you can cook right away. This guide covers that first cook, everyday cleaning, and how to bring the surface back if you spot rust or uneven patches. Cast iron builds character with use — a few simple habits keep it cooking beautifully for decades.

## What "pre-seasoned" means and your first cook

The skillet leaves our warehouse with three coats of vegetable oil baked at high heat. That initial seasoning prevents rust during shipping and gives you a head start, but it is thin. Your first few cooks will feel slightly sticky or show some light residue on the towel when you wipe the pan — that is normal.

Before the first use, rinse the skillet under warm water and dry it completely. Rub a very thin layer of neutral oil (canola, grapeseed, or vegetable) over the cooking surface with a paper towel, leaving almost no visible sheen. Heat the skillet over medium for two minutes, let it cool, then wipe out any excess. Now cook something with a bit of fat — eggs, bacon, or sautéed vegetables work well. The seasoning will darken and smooth out over the next several meals.

## Everyday cleaning: soap is fine in moderation

Rinse the skillet under hot water as soon as it is cool enough to handle. For stuck-on bits, use a stiff brush or a chain-mail scrubber and a small amount of mild dish soap. Modern dish soap will not strip seasoning the way lye-based soaps did generations ago, but you do not need much — a pea-sized drop is plenty.

Dry the pan immediately with a towel, then set it on low heat for one minute to evaporate any moisture in the pores. While it is still warm, wipe the interior with a lightly oiled paper towel. Store the skillet in a dry spot. If you stack cookware, place a paper towel between pieces to let air circulate.

Never soak cast iron overnight or run it through the dishwasher. For comparison, our Ceramic Mixing Bowl Set (FG-KIT-003) is dishwasher-safe — see our guide on Ceramic and dishwasher use for care details on that line.

## How to remove rust and re-season

Surface rust appears as orange or brown spots, usually after the pan sat wet or in humid storage. Light rust comes off with a few minutes of scrubbing.

Sprinkle coarse salt over the rusted area and scrub with half a potato or a damp cloth, applying firm pressure. Rinse, then dry the skillet completely over low heat. If rust remains, use fine steel wool or a scouring pad, working in small circles. Once the surface is smooth and rust-free, apply a thin coat of oil to the entire interior and bake the skillet upside down at 450°F for one hour, with foil on the rack below to catch drips. Let it cool in the oven. Repeat this baking step two or three times if the seasoning looks patchy. The pan will darken with each cycle.

Heavy rust that pits the metal cannot be fully removed at home, but the skillet remains safe to cook with once you rebuild the seasoning layer on top.

## FAQ

**Can I cook acidic foods like tomato sauce in cast iron?**  
Yes, but limit simmering time to under 30 minutes for the first few months. Acidic ingredients can lift seasoning if the layer is still thin. Once the pan is well-seasoned — dark, glossy, and non-stick — tomato-based dishes cause no harm.

**The surface looks blotchy after cleaning. Did I damage it?**  
Uneven color is cosmetic. Seasoning builds in layers, and some areas polymerize faster depending on heat exposure and oil distribution. Keep cooking with a bit of fat, and the finish will even out. See our cast-iron capacity guide for recipe ideas that build seasoning naturally.

**How often should I re-season the whole pan?**  
Most cooks never need to. If food starts sticking in multiple spots or the surface feels tacky after cleaning, a full oven re-season (described above) resets the layer. Otherwise, the thin oil wipe after each use maintains the finish indefinitely.
